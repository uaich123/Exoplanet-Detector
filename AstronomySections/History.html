History of Astronomy

I'll start by saying that what we know about planets is much more ancient than the 20th century. Honestly, astronomy could be one of the oldest, if not THE oldest profession in the world. At that time, any one who looked up to see the celestial bodies such as the Sun and Moon could be considered as an amateur astrononomer.

The first representations of celestial bodies were proposed during the time of the Neolithic period, very often just the sun and the moon. These representations were heavily tied to religious beliefs, leading to the creations of multiple cosmogonies that tried to explain the structure of the universe.

The model above shows the representation of the model by John Milton(1608-1674) in his epic poem Paradise Lost representing the ancient religious beliefs of the universe. As the picture above shows, the world has four main parts: Heaven, Hell, the main world, and chaos.

The main world is surrounded by chaos, and outside is heaven on the top and hell on the bottom, linked to the main world by their respective gates as shown by the respective blue lines on the top and bottom of the inner circle

There were multiple other theories like this, especially in India, but the astronomers were mainly interested in the motion: specifically, the motion of The Sun, the motion of The Moon, and the motion of The Stars.

Some interesting obervations they noticed were that all the stars weren't always setting and rising at the same time during the day. Also, the height of the sun in the sky was dependent of the period of the year. Thus, as it's pretty obvious, something was happening here. And with the glorification of nature itself, that "something" was used in funeral rituals and for burials.

Like the example, above, which already dates back to several thousands of years before Christ, there are even older examples with the same concept, such as the Tumulus of Tassili, or graves with corridors in Knowth, Ireland.

It wasn't just The Sun and The Moon! Turns out, early astrononomers noticed that there were five other objects that were moving on the background of the fixed stars. Those objects were Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn(dubbed "the wandering stars" by the greek), also the five brightest planets in our solar system. These five stars brought a whole new field of discussion.

  • Unlike stars that are "fixed" or The Sun which takes only one day to move through the sky, these planets end up taking several months! For example, Venus takes 250 days and Mars takes 465 days to cross the sky.
  • Evidenced by the above points, it's clear that these planets don't go at the same speed. Thus, there motions are independent
  • If we follow these planets on the sky, we'll see that they reverse their motion at some poin for a temporary amount of time. So they go forward , go backwards for a bit, and finally continue going forward. These can be referred to as retrograde motions

With all this new info, the idea now was to develop a model that could explain The World at that time. And at that time, The World was just Earth and what was moving around it, so really just very local in an astronomical sense.

Now, the world is moving from a cosmogony to a cosmology. We want to understand how The Universe works. Why do we see these new motions?
